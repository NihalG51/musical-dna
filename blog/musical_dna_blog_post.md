# Teaching a Computer to Hear Bach, Then Pointing It at a $7 Million Lawsuit

*A summer research project on music, code, and copyright law*

In 2015, a jury decided that "Blurred Lines" infringed on Marvin Gaye's "Got to Give It Up." The two songs don't share a melody. They don't share a chord progression. What they share is a feel: a groove, a vibe, the sense that one was clearly inspired by the other. The jury awarded Gaye's family $7.4 million anyway, and musicians have been nervous about it ever since. If you can get sued for a vibe, how do you know what you're even allowed to write?

That question stuck with me. I play music and I write code, and somewhere in between the two I started wondering whether you could actually measure this. Not "does it sound similar" in the vague, gut-feeling way a jury decides it, but literally, mathematically: extract numbers from two songs and compare them. And if you could, would those numbers agree with what courts decide, or would they tell a completely different story?

That question turned into a summer-long project I've been calling Musical DNA. It has two halves. The first teaches a computer to recognize a composer's style. Can it tell Bach from Mozart just by looking at the notes? The second turns that same toolkit on real music copyright lawsuits, to see whether "sounds similar" and "legally infringing" are actually the same thing. Spoiler: they're not, and the reason turned out to be more interesting than I expected.

## Part 1: What does Bach sound like, mathematically?

Every composer has habits. Bach loves wide-ranging counterpoint, with different melodic lines dancing far apart from each other. Debussy avoids landing firmly in any one key, which is part of why his music feels so dreamlike. Chopin writes smooth, singable melodies with almost no big jumps between notes. Musicians learn to recognize these habits by ear, over years of listening. I wanted to know if a computer could learn to recognize them from raw data instead.

So I collected 353 pieces of piano music by six composers (Bach, Mozart, Beethoven, Chopin, Debussy, and Rachmaninoff) as MIDI files, a digital format that records every note, its pitch, its timing, and its length rather than an audio recording. Then I wrote code to extract 21 measurable features from each piece: things like how wide a pitch range the piece covers, how often the key changes, how "crunchy" or dissonant the harmonies are, and how busy or sparse the rhythm is.

I fed all of that into a machine learning model, a Random Forest, which is basically a large committee of simple decision rules voting on the answer, and asked it to guess which of the six composers wrote a piece it had never seen before. It got the right answer 88.7% of the time.

More interesting than the accuracy number is what the model learned to pay attention to. The single most useful clue, by a wide margin, was pitch range, meaning how far apart the highest and lowest notes are. That tracks with real musicology: Bach's contrapuntal writing spans huge distances between voices, while Romantic composers tend to write more compact, singable lines. The model also leaned heavily on "key stability," a way of measuring how firmly a piece commits to one key. Bach is rock-solid. Debussy deliberately avoids committing, and that harmonic ambiguity is basically his whole aesthetic. The computer rediscovered, from scratch, something music theorists have been saying for a century.

Then I did something a little mean to it. I fed it AI-generated music. I asked Suno and Udio (AI music generators) to produce tracks "in the style of" each of the six composers, then ran those through my classifier to see if it would recognize the intended style. Nine out of ten, it nailed it. The tenth is the finding I like most from this half of the project. I submitted a Suno track generated in the style of Carnatic music, a completely different classical tradition from South India with no historical connection to Bach, and the model confidently identified it as Bach.

At first I thought this was a bug. It isn't. It's an important limitation, and the kind of thing you'd never find if you only tested your model on the same six European composers it was trained on. Carnatic music happens to share some surface-level traits with Baroque counterpoint, like dense ornamentation, systematic rhythmic patterns, and modal scales. My model, having never heard anything outside the European classical tradition, grabbed the nearest label it had. It wasn't recognizing "Bach-ness." It was recognizing "European-classical-ness," and Bach was just the closest bucket available. Any tool trained on a narrow slice of music will confidently misjudge anything outside that slice, which matters a lot if you want to use tools like this on culturally diverse music, or, as it turns out, on real legal disputes involving pop, rock, and hip-hop.

## Part 2: Can code predict a lawsuit?

This is where it gets interesting for anyone who has ever wondered how music copyright actually works.

I built a dataset of 43 real music copyright cases spanning 1946 to 2023: everything from George Harrison's "My Sweet Lord" (found to have subconsciously copied a 1963 girl-group song) to Ed Sheeran's "Shape of You" (accused of copying an obscure song almost nobody had heard) to, yes, "Blurred Lines." For 33 of those cases, I tracked down the actual recordings, converted them into the same note-by-note MIDI format I used for the composer project, and computed similarity scores between the plaintiff's song and the defendant's: how similar the melodies, the harmonies, the rhythms, and even short repeated phrases are.

Then I asked the obvious question. Does higher computed similarity predict a court finding infringement?

The honest answer is: barely at all. I built a model that tries to guess the outcome of a case using only these musical similarity numbers, and it performed worse than random guessing. Then I built a second model using only non-musical facts about each case: how famous the original artist was, how commercially successful the accused song became, whether an expert musicologist testified, and whether the case went to a U.S. court or settled elsewhere. That model was dramatically better, anticipating the outcome about 73 to 75% of the time. The strongest predictor of all, stronger than any musical measurement, was simply how famous the plaintiff was.

I want to be upfront about something, because it's an easy mistake to make and an important one to catch. My first version of this analysis included whether a case had settled as one of the non-musical factors, and it made the model look almost perfect. That wasn't a real finding. It was an artifact of how I'd built the dataset. Every settled case in my data had been labeled "infringement" by default, since a settlement produces no independent court ruling to check against. So the model wasn't predicting anything; it was reading a label I'd accidentally baked in twice. I caught it, pulled that feature out, and the real result, that non-musical factors still win but less dramatically, is what's in the final paper. I mention it because that's a more honest way to talk about research than only showing the clean version at the end.

The most fun part of this half of the project was going back to five specific cases where the computer's judgment and the court's judgment disagreed the most, and digging into why. Two stood out.

The first is "Shape of You." Both of my tools, the specialized similarity engine and, independently, the classical composer model applied in a completely different way, agreed that Ed Sheeran's song and the obscure track it was accused of copying are unusually similar. And yet Sheeran won. Why? Because the accused song had been played on the radio exactly twice and had about 13,000 YouTube views. The court didn't disagree that the songs sound alike. It ruled that Sheeran had essentially no way to have ever heard it. Similarity wasn't the issue. Opportunity was.

The second, and the one I keep coming back to, is "Blurred Lines" itself. I ran my classical-composer style engine, the same one that learned to recognize Bach, directly on "Got to Give It Up" and "Blurred Lines," treating them the way it would treat two piano pieces. It found them to be the least similar pair out of every case I examined this way. My specialized copyright-similarity engine agreed: nothing about the melody, harmony, or rhythm stood out as unusually close. Two tools, built completely independently, for completely different purposes, neither designed with this lawsuit in mind, landed in the same place. By any measurable standard I could construct, these two songs aren't that alike. That lines up with what musicologists have been saying since the verdict came down: the jury was reacting to a feel, a groove, an era, rather than to anything you could point to on a page of sheet music. It was oddly satisfying to get quantitative backup for an argument I'd only ever seen made qualitatively.

## What I actually learned

Going in, I expected the interesting result to be a better similarity algorithm. What I found was almost the opposite. Similarity, measured as carefully as I could measure it, mostly isn't what decides these cases. Fame is. Access is. Which court you're in is. That isn't a knock on the legal system, since access and fame are legitimate parts of copyright law rather than made-up excuses, but it does mean that "does this sound like that" is a much smaller piece of the puzzle than I assumed before I started.

I also learned, the hard way, that it's worth actively hunting for the moment your own analysis looks too good, because that's usually where you've made a mistake rather than a discovery. And I learned that a tool built for one narrow purpose, telling six long-dead European composers apart, can still say something real when you point it, carefully and with the right caveats, at a question nobody built it to answer.
